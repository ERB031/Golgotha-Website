Director’s Statement

Horror as poetry, memory as scripture.

I direct Golgotha as a visual symphony, treating dread like a composition of silence, ritual, and sudden light. Each frame leans into the tension between brutal reality and metaphysical suggestion.

Writer / Director

Influences

  • David Lynch’s dream-logic
  • Robert Eggers’s devotion to ritual
  • Ari Aster’s psychological excavation

Our palette recalls decayed cathedrals and nocturnal alleyways, bending the city into a living reliquary. Grief, faith, and memory become characters that haunt Detective Hill until he surrenders to transformation.

Every location is treated like a sacred site defaced by modern neglect. We shoot through layers of smoke, rain, and glass to trap our detective between worlds—presenting horror as a prayer answered in whispers.

The film’s tone is restrained yet indelible. Stillness holds as much power as violence. We move the camera like a slow procession, letting the audience feel the echo of loss before the next revelation erupts.
